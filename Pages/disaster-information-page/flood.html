Flood

In the Philippines, flooding is a frequent and serious natural hazard that frequently results from monsoon weather patterns, tropical cyclones, or high, protracted rainfall. PAGASA defines a flood as “an abnormal, progressive rise in the water level of a stream that may result in the overflowing with the subsequent inundation of areas which are not normally submerged.” To put it more simply, floods happen when water builds up from rain or overflows from rivers or drainage systems, causing water to spread into adjacent land. [Source: PAGASA]

The Philippines is especially susceptible to flooding due to a number of variables. Tropical cyclones, thunderstorms, the northeast and southwest monsoons, and frontal systems are examples of meteorological causes. Physically, flood risk is increased by increasing urbanization, particularly uncontrolled growth on riverbanks and floodplains, poor or insufficient drainage systems, and environmental degradation (such as river silting and forest cover loss). Furthermore, low-lying coastal communities are more vulnerable to flooding due to rising sea levels.

PAGASA's Flood Forecasting and Warning System (FFWS) oversees flood forecasting and warning in the Philippines. Depending on the degree of anticipated flooding, they run streamflow simulations, gather real-time hydrometeorological data, and issue various Flood Bulletins, such as Flood Outlook, Advisory, Warning, and Critical Flood Warning. In order to provide advisories and coordinate reaction, PAGASA also collaborates with the Office of Civil Defense (OCD), local government entities, and dam offices. [Source: PAGASA]

In the Philippines, floods can have catastrophic effects. Homes, neighborhoods, infrastructure, and agriculture can all be destroyed by floodwaters. Days may pass during significant flooding incidents, particularly in river basins where water builds up and gradually recedes. The nation experiences both seasonal flooding and flash floods due to its geographic and climatic characteristics, making early warning systems and readiness essential. Flood risks are expected to increase as a result of rising rainfall trends brought on by climate change, particularly during the monsoon seasons.
