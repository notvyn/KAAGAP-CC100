Landslide

The mass movement of rock, soil, and debris down a slope caused by gravityâ€”that is, when the forces dragging material downhill outweigh the forces maintaining the slope's stability is referred to as a landslide. Depending on the steepness of the slope, the type of soil or rock, and the triggering circumstances, landslides can occur gradually over time or abruptly and quickly, resulting in rockfalls, debris flows, or slope failure.

Landslides are somewhat frequent in the Philippines due to a mix of man-made and natural causes. Intense or persistent rainfall, which is frequently associated with typhoons and monsoon weather systems, is one of the natural triggers that saturates soil and weakens slope stability. Because of its location along the Pacific Ring of Fire, the nation frequently experiences strong earthquakes that can fracture or shake slopes, causing landslides. Volcanic ash and tephra can readily become unstable when wet, making volcano-derived terrain such as loose volcanic deposits or soft volcanic soil especially hazardous. By diminishing natural support structures, human activities like deforestation, uncontrolled land use (such building on slopes), quarrying, inadequate drainage, and slope-modifying construction raise the danger of slope failure. [Source: PHIVOLCS]

In the Philippines, some of the regions most vulnerable to landslides are:

Mountain Province, Benguet, Apayao, and Nueva Vizcaya are examples of mountainous and highland provinces where steep slopes, high elevation, and significant rainfall converge.

Certain regions of the Visayas and Mindanao, particularly those with high or volcanic terrain, as well as more specialized areas like Southern Leyte, which has historically seen devastating landslides brought on by a combination of rainfall, steep slopes, geological faults, and deteriorated vegetation cover.

Even smaller towns or isolated settlements in mountainous or hilly areas, particularly those close to river valleys, volcanic slopes, or deforested hills, may be at danger for landslides because the risk is mostly dependent on slope steepness, soil/rock type, rainfall intensity, and human activity. For this reason, local-level geohazard assessments and hazard mapping are crucial. [Source: UPLB]

Being ready is crucial in light of these threats. To lessen the risk of landslides, organizations like PHIVOLCS support hazard mapping, early warning systems, and slope-protection engineering techniques (retaining walls, drainage, reforestation). Particularly during rainy seasons or following earthquakes, communities in high-risk locations should have evacuation or contingency plans and be aware of warning signals such as leaning trees, fractures on slopes, and unusual soil movement.
