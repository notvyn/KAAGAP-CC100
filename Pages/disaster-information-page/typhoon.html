Typhoon

In the Philippines, typhoons, also referred to as "bagyo," are one of the biggest natural disasters. The nation is more susceptible to tropical storms than many other regions of the world since it is located inside the Philippine Area of Responsibility (PAR). Approximately eight to nine of the 20 tropical cyclones that PAGASA (the Philippine meteorological bureau) tracks each year make landfall in the Philippines. Approximately 70% of the storms that impact the nation develop during typhoon season, which peaks between July and October.

Typhoons are classified by their wind speed using the PAGASA system. For instance, sustained winds of at least 185km/h are considered a “Super typhoon” in the Philippines. These strong stroms are especially destructive in low-trying coastal and mountainous areas because they can cause landslides, flooding, storm stooges, torrential rainfall, and strong winds. Building resilience requires an understanding of these trends and the dangers they present. People and communities may better prepare and respond to typhoons by understanding how they form, how they are measured, and what kind of damage they can do. Knowing when to evacuate, how to strengthen homes, and where to find trustworthy, current forecasts and warnings from PAGASA and local authorities are all examples of this.

Signs and Warnings

When a typhoon is approaching the Philippines, there are several signs that people may start to notice even before a formal warning is issued. These include sudden changes in wind behavior, including a blast of wind, a change in direction, or an increase in speed. Rapid cloud accumulation, darkening skies, and increased rainfall are typical early warning indicators. In coastal areas, sea levels may rise, resulting in storm surges and noticeably stronger waves. Together with regular weather information, these natural cues should encourage communities to stay alert.

The Tropical Cyclone Wind Signal (TCWS), which is kept up to date by PAGASA, is the main official typhoon warning system in the Philippines. Each of the five signal levels (1 - 5) represents increasing wind intensity and shorter lead times for the anticipated winds.

  • Signal 1 means winds of ~39-61 km/h are expected within about 36 hours.
  • Signal 2 signals winds of ~62-88 km/h within 24 hours.
  • Signal 3 means ~89-117 km/h within 18 hours.
  • Signal 4 indicates stronger typhoon winds: ~118-184 km/h expected within 12 hours.
  • Signal 5 the highest warns of winds 185 km/h or more within 12 hours, and represents an extremely dangerous, life-threatening situation.

There are suggested actions and anticipated effects for every signal level. Higher signals, for instance, have the potential to seriously harm light structures, turn loose things into projectiles, and increase the likelihood of power outages, telecom interruptions, and flying debris. PAGASA issues a warning of "catastrophic" damage when Signal No. 5 is activated, advising prompt evacuation of susceptible regions and stringent home security measures.

[Source: PAGASA, PAGASA, and GMA Network]
