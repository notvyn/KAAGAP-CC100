Volcanic Eruption

Because the Philippines is situated along the Pacific Ring of Fire, a tectonically active region where plates converge and magma can reach the surface, volcanic eruptions are strong natural occurrences. Molten rock (lava), ash, gas, and broken rock (tephra) can all be released during a volcano eruption, posing major hazards to neighboring communities. The Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS) keeps a careful eye on these active volcanoes in order to detect any indications of instability, send out alerts, and lower the danger of volcanic eruptions.

According to PHIVOLCS, the Philippines has between 23 and 24 active volcanoes. Mount Mayon, Taal, Pinatubo, Kanlaon, and Bulusan are notable examples. Some of these volcanoes have explosive eruptions that produce pyroclastic flows and huge ash plumes, while others may have slower lava flows or more passive activity.

There are many different and potentially very hazardous volcanic risks. These include volcanic tsunamis, pyroclastic density currents, ashfall, lava flows, ballistic projectiles, hazardous gasses, and lahars. The areas closest to active volcanoes are the most vulnerable to volcanic dangers. For instance, communities in the vicinity of the Mayon Volcano (Albay), Taal Caldera and the towns that surround it (Batangas and Cavite), Kanlaon (Negros Occidental and Negros Oriental), Bulusan's neighboring barangays (Sorsogon), and the Pinatubo river basins (Pampanga, Tarlac, Zambales) are frequently at risk from eruptions, lethal ashfall, and lahar flows. These areas are routinely monitored for gas emissions, seismic activity, and ground deformation and are included in PHIVOLCS danger maps.

Being ready and keeping an eye on things are crucial. PHIVOLCS communicates risk to local governments and populations through an alert-level system that ranges from low-level unrest to an impending hazardous eruption. To increase public access to volcanic status, historical data, and warning alerts, PHIVOLCS introduced digital tools in 2025 such as the VolcanoPH Info App and VOLCAN, a near-real-time visual and seismic data platform. The Philippines hopes to increase resilience and lessen the devastating effects of volcanic eruptions by integrating scientific monitoring, public education, and early warning systems. [Source: DOST]
