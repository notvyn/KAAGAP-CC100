After a Disaster

Being ready for a crisis can significantly lower risks, save lives, and increase community resilience. To help you keep prepared, stay safe, and react appropriately in any situation, this section offers crucial advice and doable actions.

The first thing to do after a disaster, such as a typhoon, flood, earthquake, or volcanic eruption, is to wait for government approval before going back home or re-entering the damaged areas. The location should be deemed safe by local authorities or the Office of Civil Defense (OCD) or the National Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council (NDRRMC). This guarantees that risks like downed power lines, unstable buildings, or tainted water are taken care of first.

Examine the area around your house for hazards and damage once it is safe to return. Keep an eye out for sanitary problems, exposed electrical wires, gas or water leaks, and structural damage. When investigating, use a flashlight rather than an open flame, and wait until an electrician or other qualified individual has examined the wiring and outlets before turning on the electricity. Until a clean supply is certified, boil or purify drinking water and discard any potentially contaminated food or water. Avoiding "sight-seeing" or going into restricted areas is equally crucial in order to prevent interference with emergency, rescue, and clearing efforts.

Physical cleanup is only one aspect of recovery; other aspects include community support, documentation, and wellness. Notify local authorities or disaster aid organizations of any damage, particularly if homes, utilities, or infrastructure are impacted. Register for aid or relief supplies through your local government or NDRRMC/OCD-coordinated disaster response teams if you are eligible. Families should also make sure they have access to sanitary facilities, clean water, and healthcare. Hygiene, clean water, and medical examinations are essential before returning to regular life after catastrophes since flooding, debris, or damage to water systems can cause water-borne illnesses or other health problems.

In the end, disasters are community-wide occurrences rather than only personal difficulties. Collaborate with recovery programs, assist neighbors (particularly the elderly, children, or people with disabilities), and rely on community cooperation through local disaster risk reduction units. The significance of coordinated, community-based rehabilitation and recovery initiatives is emphasized in the updated Ready to Rebuild: Disaster Rehabilitation and Recovery Planning Guide, which is utilized by both national and local governments.
