During a Disaster

The most crucial thing to do in the event of a disaster, be it a typhoon, flood, earthquake, or volcanic eruption, is to remain composed, stay informed, and adhere to government directives. Pay attention to reliable sources like your local government, PAGASA, PHIVOLCS, the Office of Civil Defense (OCD), and emergency announcements. Even in the event of a power or internet outage, you can still get alerts, evacuation orders, and real-time information provided you have a charged radio or cell phone (with a power bank).

Make sure your emergency pack, or "go-bag," is ready in case you need to flee or stay put ideally, it should be ready before a disaster strikes. Clean water, non-perishable food, flashlights with additional batteries, a first-aid kit, essential medications, official documents (in a waterproof container), and basic hygiene supplies should all be included in this bag. You and your family can survive the first crucial 48 to 72 hours if you have this on hand.

Avoid needless exposure to danger during the tragedy. Avoid driving or wading through floodwaters, stay away from windows and unstable buildings during storms or earthquakes, and avoid coastal locations if there is a tsunami or storm surge risk. If you are told to evacuate, do so as soon as possible to avoid major flooding or blocked roadways.

Continue to adhere to official directions after the initial hit before going back home. Hazards like downed electrical wires, gas leaks, weakened structures, tainted water, or increasing flood levels can always be dangerous even after the immediate threat appears to have passed. In the event of a power outage, use battery-operated flashlights rather than open fires, stay away from food and water contaminated by floodwater, and notify your local disaster response authorities of any damaged infrastructure.
