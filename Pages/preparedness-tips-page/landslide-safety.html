Landslide Safety

Take precautions to lower your risk of landslides before heavy rains or if you live in a mountainous or sloping location. Examine government hazard maps (such as PHIVOLCS "Landslide Hazard Maps" or hazard-assessment tools) to determine whether your house or neighborhood is in a landslide-prone area. Avoid constructing or planting permanent structures close to streams, steep slopes, or areas where landslides have occurred in the past. Additionally, pack an emergency "go-bag" with necessities such as non-perishable food, bottled water, a torch, a first aid kit, rain clothing, sturdy shoes, critical documents in a waterproof container, and a battery-operated radio or alert system.

Keep an eye out for indicators of slope instability, such as unexpected ground fissures, tilting trees or structures, bulging soil, or an abrupt rise in mud-colored water flow in neighboring streams, or when heavy rain begins. Evacuate right away to higher, safer ground if local authorities or community disaster-risk offices issue a landslide or rainstorm alarm. Avoid the route of debris, cover your head, and remain away from susceptible slopes and streams if evacuation is not an option and a landslide starts.

Don't go back to impacted slopes after a landslide or after a period of intense rain until the local authorities have declared the region safe. If you have to go back, take a close look at your home and its surroundings. Examine the walls, foundation, and drainage, and keep an eye out for any indications of additional earth movement. Assist your neighbors, particularly the elderly, children, and those with disabilities, and notify the appropriate authorities of any damaged roads, fallen trees, or downed power lines. Long-term, to lower the risk of future landslides, think about promoting or supporting slope-stabilization techniques like reforestation, appropriate drainage, retaining walls, or slope engineering.
