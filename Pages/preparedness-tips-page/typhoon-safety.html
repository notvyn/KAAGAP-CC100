Typhoon Safety

Being ready is your first line of defense when a typhoon is on its way. Make sure you keep informed before the storm arrives by following official weather advisories and routinely monitoring bulletins from PAGASA or local government units (LGUs). To begin, assemble an emergency "go-bag" with necessities such as drinking water, non-perishable food, flashlights with extra batteries, a first aid kit, power banks, face masks, hygiene products, basic medications, waterproof containers for important documents, and enough cash to last three to five days. [Source: Homecredit]

Next, take action to safeguard your house and lessen its susceptibility to flooding, rain, and severe winds. Tighten or fix loose doors, windows, and roofs. Drying racks, potted plants, and other lightweight items that could blow in high winds should be brought inside or securely tied down outside. Cut off any tree branches that dangle over the edge and could fall. Raising valuables and electronics above anticipated flood levels is advised if your neighborhood is prone to flooding. Additionally, plan evacuation routes and locate the closest evacuation center in advance, particularly if local advisories or typhoon-signal alerts require evacuation. [Source: Proudbisayabai]

Prioritize safety during the storm by staying inside, avoiding windows and glass doors, and staying in the safest area of the home. To lower the risk of electrocution, unplug electrical appliances in the event of floods. To get real-time updates from PAGASA or local authorities, have your phone, radio, or battery-operated devices close at hand. Avoid going near floodwaters and don't try to drive or walk across them if there is a chance of flooding or a storm surge.

Examine your surroundings for structural damage, downed power lines, gas leaks, or flood dangers before venturing outside after the typhoon has passed and officials have pronounced it safe. If returning home after evacuation, ensure the house, utilities, and water supply are safe before resuming normal use. If you can, assist your neighbors, especially the elderly, children, and those with disabilities. You should also notify the local hotlines or disaster response offices of any crises.
